Explainers

    
Why does a near-full drive slow my computer down?
      
Windows constantly needs free space for the page file (virtual memory), temporary files, and updates. When a drive — especially an SSD — climbs above roughly 85–90% full, the system has less room to work and write performance drops. Freeing space back below ~85% usually restores normal responsiveness. The goal isn't just to fit a new file; it's to leave breathing room.

    

    
What's actually safe to delete?
      
Temporary files, the Recycle Bin, Windows Update leftovers, and most app caches are safe — apps regenerate them as needed. Your Downloads folder is "review" because it's your files. Never delete things in Windows, Program Files, or driver folders unless you know exactly what they are. This tool flags risk levels so you don't have to guess.

    

    
What are "temp" and "cache" folders?
      
Temp folders hold short-lived working files that programs forget to clean up. Caches are saved copies of data (web pages, thumbnails, package downloads) kept around to make things faster next time. Both are safe to clear — you'll just see a one-time slowdown as they rebuild.

    

    
Is hosting this page publicly really safe?
      
Yes. The page is a static shell with no data in it. Your scan output is pasted in your browser and processed locally — it's never uploaded because the page makes no network calls at all. The only data that could ever be sensitive is folder names, and those stay on your machine. Use the "Redact" toggle if you want even folder paths and your machine name stripped from the scan output.

    

    
How do I undo a cleanup?
      
Cache and temp deletions can't be "undone," but they're designed to be disposable — Windows and your apps rebuild them automatically. Items sent to the Recycle Bin can be restored until you empty it. Uninstalled programs can be reinstalled. Nothing this tool suggests touches your personal documents.
